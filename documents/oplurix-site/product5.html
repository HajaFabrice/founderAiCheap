Section 1

The Core Promise

Somewhere, right now, there are field notebooks from surveys conducted in the 1990s, 2000s, or 2010s that contain irreplaceable biodiversity observations. Some are in institutional archives. Many are in the personal possession of researchers who never had the time or the system to digitize them. A significant portion will never be digitized at all.

This is not a technology problem. Scanning, OCR, and AI tools are better than they have ever been. It is a workflow problem. There is no clear, repeatable process that takes a researcher from a stack of handwritten notebooks to a clean, documented, analysis-ready dataset — without making errors, losing data, or spending six months on what should take six days.

This guide is that workflow. It works for a single notebook from last field season. It works for twenty years of handwritten lemur behavioural observations. It works for data in French, Malagasy, or English. It works whether your handwriting is neat or whether it is the kind of field shorthand only you can read.

Section 2

10 Real Pain Points

01
You do not know where to start.

You have a stack of notebooks. Some are organised. Some are not. Some have species codes only you remember. You open the first one and immediately feel overwhelmed. The workflow in this guide removes that paralysis by giving you a decision at each step, not a blank page.

02
OCR fails on field handwriting.

Standard OCR software was trained on printed text. Field notebooks contain abbreviations, crossed-out entries, arrows, side notes, illegible species codes, smudged ink, and condition damage. OCR alone is insufficient — it must be combined with AI interpretation and manual verification. This guide specifies exactly when to use which.

03
You introduce transcription errors while trying to fix the data.

Manual transcription from paper to spreadsheet produces approximately 1 error per 300 keystrokes under normal conditions. Over a large dataset, this is hundreds of corrupted records. The workflow in this guide layers three independent validation steps to catch these before the data is used in any analysis.

04
Your column names and data formats are inconsistent across notebooks.

Season 1 uses "sp_code." Season 2 uses "Species." Season 3 uses "Spp." The date format changes three times. GPS format shifts from degrees-minutes-seconds to decimal degrees midway through a notebook. The Column Standardisation Library in Section 9 resolves all of these systematically.

05
You cannot tell which records are certain and which are questionable.

In the field, observations have varying levels of confidence. A confident species ID written clearly is not the same as a tentative ID written with a question mark and a note about poor light. Without a confidence flag in your dataset, these are indistinguishable from each other.

06
The physical notebooks are deteriorating.

Humidity, insects, mould, and light damage handwritten records faster than most researchers expect. Notebooks from 10–15 years ago in tropical storage conditions are often partially illegible. This guide includes an urgent-first triage system for deteriorating records.

07
You have your own shorthand that nobody else can decode.

Field shorthand is a legitimate and efficient system in the field. In a digital dataset, "lm cf juv 2x rt bank" is not a data record — it is an encoding problem. The workflow guides you through building a personal codebook that translates your shorthand into structured fields.

08
You do not know how to handle genuinely illegible sections.

Every notebook has sections that cannot be read with confidence. The correct response is not to guess and not to omit — it is to flag the record explicitly with an illegibility code, preserve what is readable, and document what is missing. The Error Taxonomy in Section 8 covers every category of illegibility.

09
You have no plan for long-term preservation of the physical notebooks.

Digitizing is not the same as archiving. The workflow in this guide produces both a verified digital dataset and a properly archived physical notebook — with a documented relationship between the two.

10
You cannot verify the accuracy of your own transcription.

Checking your own work is cognitively difficult — your brain reads what it expects to see. This guide includes structured cross-checking protocols that use a second person, an AI tool, or a systematic time-delayed re-read to catch the errors that the transcriber misses.

1

TRIAGE AND ASSESS

Before digitizing anything, assess the full set of notebooks. This takes 30–60 minutes and determines the order of work and the tools required.

Triage Assessment Questions — for each notebook
  • Physical condition: Good / Fair (minor damage) / Poor (significant deterioration) / Critical (immediate risk of loss)
  • Handwriting legibility: Clear / Mixed / Difficult / Requires specialist knowledge of author's shorthand
  • Structure: Does the notebook use a consistent format page-to-page, or is it free-form?
  • Language: English / French / Malagasy / Mixed / Code-heavy
  • Data types present: Species observations / Transect data / GPS waypoints / Behavioural records / Environmental measurements / Mixed
  • Priority: Is this data already used in a publication, or is it unpublished baseline data?

Output of Step 1: A triage log. One row per notebook. Columns: notebook ID, date range, condition, legibility, priority, estimated digitization time.

Critical priority rule

Digitize "Critical" condition notebooks FIRST, regardless of scientific priority. Physical deterioration is irreversible. A notebook you cannot read next year is more urgent than a legible notebook from last season.

2

BUILD THE CODEBOOK

Before transcribing a single observation, document every abbreviation, symbol, and code used in the notebook. This is the most commonly skipped step — and the one that causes the most errors downstream.

Codebook Template

For each code, abbreviation, or symbol in the notebook, create one row:

  • Code as written: exactly as it appears in the notebook (e.g., "Lc", "juv", "rt bank", "x3", "?")
  • Meaning: the full, unambiguous interpretation (e.g., "Lemur catta", "juvenile", "right river bank", "three individuals", "uncertain identification")
  • Digital field: which column in the final dataset this maps to (e.g., "species_code", "age_class", "position", "count", "confidence_flag")
  • Digital value: the standardised value to enter in that field (e.g., "LEMURCATTA", "JUV", "RIGHT_BANK", "3", "UNCERTAIN")

Output of Step 2: A codebook CSV or sheet. Keep it open throughout the entire transcription process.

Use Prompt 1 in the AI Prompt Pack (Section 6) to help build the codebook if you have access to a legible overview of the notebook's contents. The AI can suggest likely interpretations for common field abbreviations — but you must verify every entry against the actual notebook.

3

SCAN AND OCR

Create a high-resolution digital image of every page before any manual transcription. This is your archival copy and your OCR source. The scan comes before the transcription — always.

Scanning Specifications
  • Resolution: 300 DPI minimum for OCR. 600 DPI for damaged or faded notebooks. 1200 DPI for notebooks with very small handwriting or fine detail.
  • Format: Save as high-quality JPG (for storage efficiency) or TIFF (for maximum quality preservation).
  • Colour mode: Colour scan, not greyscale — even for pencil notebooks. Colour captures ink degradation, water damage, and annotation colour-coding.
  • Naming convention: NotebookID_PageNumber_Date.jpg (e.g., NB001_P042_2026-05-01.jpg). Never use spaces in filenames.
  • Equipment: Flatbed scanner preferred. Document camera acceptable. Phone camera with a scanner app (Microsoft Lens, Adobe Scan) is usable for Good-condition notebooks.
OCR tool selection

For printed field forms with handwritten entries: Adobe Acrobat OCR, ABBYY FineReader. These handle structured documents with mixed print/handwriting reasonably well.

For fully handwritten notebooks: Google Document AI, AWS Textract, or Microsoft Azure Document Intelligence. These are cloud-based services with free tiers adequate for most field notebook volumes.

For any notebook: AI-assisted transcription (GPT-4 Vision, Claude) is often more accurate than pure OCR for field handwriting. See Prompt 2. Use this as a complement to OCR, not a replacement for verification.

Output of Step 3: (a) Complete scan archive, one folder per notebook. (b) OCR raw output text file for each page. Do not clean the OCR output yet — preserve it as a reference.

4

TRANSCRIBE AND CLEAN

The transcription phase produces the first version of the digital dataset. Work page by page, using the codebook (Step 2) and the scanned images (Step 3) simultaneously. Never transcribe from the physical notebook at this stage — always from the scan.

Transcription Rules
  • One row per observation. Never combine two observations in one row.
  • Use the standardised values from the codebook, not the original shorthand.
  • Add a source_page column: the scan filename or page number the record was taken from.
  • Add a confidence column: HIGH (clearly legible, certain identification) / MEDIUM (legible, uncertain ID) / LOW (partially illegible) / ILLEGIBLE (cannot be transcribed).
  • Add a transcriber_note column: for any record requiring explanation or flagging. Leave blank if no note needed.
  • For genuinely illegible records: enter "ILLEGIBLE" in every field that cannot be read. Do not leave blank — blank fields are ambiguous. ILLEGIBLE is unambiguous.

Use Prompts 3 and 4 from the AI Prompt Pack to assist with difficult sections — species name disambiguation, date format correction, and contextual interpretation of ambiguous entries.

Output of Step 4: Raw transcription spreadsheet — unchecked but complete. One row per observation. All columns populated (with ILLEGIBLE where required).

5

VALIDATE — THREE INDEPENDENT CHECKS

Never skip validation. The three checks are designed to catch different categories of error and must be performed in sequence.

Check 1 — Automated structural validation

Run the validation prompt (Prompt 7) or use Excel/Google Sheets data validation rules to check: (a) date formats are consistent, (b) GPS coordinates are in the correct range for your study area, (c) species codes are all in the master species list, (d) count fields contain only integers or ILLEGIBLE, (e) no required field is blank.

Check 2 — Cross-reference against original scan

Take 10% of records at random. Open the scan for each record. Verify that every field in the dataset matches what is written on the page. If error rate exceeds 2%, re-check the entire dataset. If error rate is below 2%, document it and proceed.

Check 3 — Ecological plausibility review

Review the dataset for records that are ecologically implausible: a species outside its known range, a count of 500 individuals for a solitary species, a time-of-day entry for a strictly nocturnal species observed at noon. These are not necessarily errors — they may be genuine rare records. Flag them for expert review rather than deleting them.

Output of Step 5: Validated dataset with a validation log documenting all errors found and how they were resolved.

6

ARCHIVE — PHYSICAL AND DIGITAL

Digitization is not archiving. Both the physical notebook and the digital files require systematic long-term storage with a documented link between them.

Physical notebook archiving
  • Store each notebook in an individual acid-free paper envelope or archival sleeve.
  • Label the envelope with: notebook ID, date range, survey area, name of field researcher, digitization date, digital file location.
  • Store in a cool, dry, dark location. Avoid cardboard boxes — use archival boxes where possible.
  • Do not store rubber-banded or under pressure. Pages must lay flat.
Digital file archiving — the 3-2-1 rule
  • 3 copies: The working copy on your computer, a backup on an external drive, and a third copy in cloud storage (Google Drive, Zenodo, institutional repository).
  • 2 storage types: Computer hard drive + external SSD or USB drive.
  • 1 offsite: Cloud storage counts as offsite. An institutional server counts as offsite.

Output of Step 6: Archived physical notebook + archived digital package (scans, codebook, raw transcription, validated dataset, validation log, metadata file).

Section 5

Tool Stack Guide

Every tool needed for the workflow, organised by function, with cost, platform, and field-specific notes. All free options are indicated.

Microsoft Lens / Adobe Scan
FREE · iOS + Android
Use for
Mobile scanning of Good-condition notebooks. Auto-crops, corrects perspective, adjusts contrast.
Strengths
Fast, free, works offline, produces PDF or JPG. Microsoft Lens integrates with OneDrive.
Limitations
Not sufficient for Critical-condition notebooks. Phone camera resolution varies. Lighting sensitivity.
Field note
Use in landscape mode with the notebook flat. Natural light from a window is better than flash.
Google Document AI / AWS Textract
Free tier available · Cloud-based
Use for
OCR on field handwriting. Significantly more accurate than offline OCR on handwritten text.
Strengths
Handles mixed print/handwriting. Returns structured JSON with confidence scores per word.
Limitations
Requires internet. Data is sent to a cloud provider — check your data sharing agreements before using. Free tier: ~1,000 pages/month.
Alternative
Tesseract OCR (free, offline, open-source) — lower accuracy on handwriting but no data sharing concern.
Claude / GPT-4 Vision (AI transcription assistant)
Free tier / ~$20/month
Use for
Interpreting difficult handwriting, resolving abbreviations, converting raw OCR output to structured fields.
Strengths
Contextual interpretation. Can use the codebook as context. Handles mixed-language entries. Much better than OCR alone for field shorthand.
Critical limitation
AI transcription must be verified. The AI will make plausible-sounding errors — especially for species names and numerical values. Never use AI output without cross-checking against the scan.
Data privacy
Do not upload sensitive unpublished data (species locations, community partner details) to commercial AI services without consent.
Google Sheets / Microsoft Excel
Free / included in Microsoft 365
Use for
The transcription spreadsheet. Data validation rules. The codebook. The validation log.
Key features
Data Validation (dropdown lists for standardised values), Conditional Formatting (flag outliers), VLOOKUP / XLOOKUP (check species codes against master list).
Field note
Work in Google Sheets if multiple people are transcribing simultaneously. Excel is better for offline work in the field.
R / Python (pandas)
FREE · Cross-platform
Use for
Automated validation checks. Merging multiple notebook datasets. Detecting outliers. Generating the validation report.
Key functions
R: readr, dplyr, validate package. Python: pandas, great_expectations library for data validation.
When needed
For datasets larger than ~2,000 rows, or when merging 5+ notebooks, manual spreadsheet validation becomes impractical. Use R or Python at that scale.
Zenodo / Figshare
FREE · Long-term data repository
Use for
Permanent archiving of digitized datasets. Generates a DOI (digital object identifier) for citability. Required by many funders for data management plans.
Strengths
Free. Persistent. Findable. Generates a DOI. CERN-maintained (Zenodo) — will not disappear.
Access control
Can be set to open access, restricted access (requires permission request), or embargoed (access opens after a set date). Use restricted or embargoed for unpublished data.

Section 10

Quality Control Checklist

Run this checklist at the end of every notebook digitization before the dataset is used in any analysis.

Completeness

  • Every page of the notebook has been scanned. Page count in scan archive matches page count in physical notebook.
  • Every observation on every page has a corresponding row in the dataset. No observations have been silently omitted.
  • Every illegible or uncertain field is explicitly marked (ILLEGIBLE, MISSING, LOW confidence) — not left blank.
  • The codebook covers every abbreviation that appears in the notebook.

Structural Integrity

  • All date fields are in YYYY-MM-DD format.
  • All time fields are in HH:MM 24-hour format.
  • All GPS coordinates are in decimal degrees, WGS84, with 5 decimal places (or flagged as ILLEGIBLE/MISSING).
  • All species codes appear in the master species list or are flagged as AMBIGUOUS.
  • All confidence values are from the controlled vocabulary: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW / ILLEGIBLE.
  • No required column contains a blank cell (ILLEGIBLE or MISSING are acceptable — blank is not).
  • record_id values are unique across the entire dataset.

Ecological Plausibility

  • All GPS coordinates fall within the bounding box of the study area (or are flagged as SPATIAL_OUTLIER).
  • Species recorded are within known ranges (or are flagged as RANGE_OUTLIER with a note).
  • Count values are plausible for the species and survey method (or are flagged and noted).
  • Time values are plausible for the species (nocturnal species at night, diurnal at day — outliers are flagged).

Cross-Reference Validation

  • 10% random sample cross-checked against original scans. Error rate documented in validation log.
  • Any error rate above 2% triggers a full re-check of the entire dataset.
  • Validation log is complete: lists every error found, how it was classified, and how it was resolved.

Archive Readiness

  • Scan archive is complete and all files are named according to the naming convention.
  • Codebook file is finalised and saved alongside the dataset.
  • Metadata file is complete (see Prompt 9 in Section 6).
  • Physical notebook is ready for archival storage (acid-free envelope, labelled correctly).
  • 3-2-1 backup completed for the digital package.

Section 12

Quick Start — Your First Notebook in 6 Hours

Hour 1 — Triage and Setup

  • Lay out all notebooks. Complete a triage assessment row for each one.
  • Identify your most urgent notebook (Critical condition) or your highest-priority notebook (most needed for current analysis).
  • Set up your folder structure for the digital archive package.
  • Open a new spreadsheet. Create the column headers from the Column Standardisation Library (Section 9) for your data type.

Hour 2 — Scan and Codebook

  • Scan all pages of the selected notebook. Name files according to the naming convention.
  • Browse through the scans to identify all abbreviations, codes, and symbols.
  • Use Prompt 1 to help draft the codebook. Verify every suggestion against the scans.
  • Save the codebook as a separate CSV file.

Hours 3–4 — Transcription

  • Work page by page from the scans (not the physical notebook).
  • Use Prompt 2 for any difficult pages — attach the scan image to the AI.
  • Fill every field for every observation. Use ILLEGIBLE or MISSING — never blank.
  • Use Prompt 3 for ambiguous species names. Use Prompt 4 for date standardisation.

Hour 5 — Validation

  • Run Prompt 7 on the complete dataset for automated structural checks.
  • Cross-check 10% of records against scans. Document the error rate.
  • Review all LOW and ILLEGIBLE confidence records for ecological plausibility.
  • Complete the validation log.

Hour 6 — Archive

  • Generate the metadata file using Prompt 9.
  • Label the physical notebook envelope and store it correctly.
  • Complete the 3-2-1 digital backup.
  • Run the Quality Control Checklist from Section 10.
  • Add a row to your triage log: this notebook is now COMPLETE.
