What is inside

Six checks. Three minutes. Run it before every post.

01
Species location sensitivity Does this post include any detail that could let someone locate a nest site, roost, den, or individual animal? GPS coordinates, named micro-sites, or recognisable habitat features all count.
02
Unpublished data protection Are you sharing specific quantitative results from work that has not yet been peer-reviewed? Numbers, indices, and statistical values belong in the paper — not in a LinkedIn post.
03
Community and partner protection Have you identified community members, local research technicians, or partner organisations without their explicit consent? General descriptors protect people without losing the story.
04
Scientific integrity Are you clearly distinguishing between observation, interpretation, and conclusion? Conservation professionals are held to a higher standard than general social media users.
05
Sensitive timing Is this project in an active negotiation, regulatory review, or live field season phase? Posting about current-phase results can disrupt the project itself.
06
Tone and confidence calibration Does the post sound like you — professional, honest, specific — or does it drift toward either overclaiming or performative modesty? Both undermine credibility differently.
Why this exists

I almost made a mistake I could not undo.

In 2017 I was writing a LinkedIn post about Zonosaurus observations in Kirindy forest — my first serious attempt at sharing field work publicly. I nearly included the GPS coordinates of a study site where we had documented unusual resting behaviour that might indicate an important microhabitat.

Not because I was careless. Because I had not thought through what a determined commercial collector could do with that information, combined with the species identification and the observation details already in the post.

"I caught it before I posted. But it was close. And nobody had ever taught me to check for it — not in my degree, not in any field training, not in any communication course."

That near-miss is why I built the ethics checklist. Six questions. Three minutes. It catches the thing you almost missed — because you were thinking about the science, not about what the information enables.

It is free because every conservation professional who shares their work publicly needs it — not as a premium product, but as a minimum standard.

Who this is for

You share field work on LinkedIn or other public platforms

You have been told to "be careful" but never told exactly what to check
