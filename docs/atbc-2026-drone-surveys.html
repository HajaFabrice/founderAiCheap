Research campaign

Thermal drone surveys in under-monitored forests in Madagascar, now heading to ATBC 2026.

Some forests in Madagascar receive very little formal monitoring. Recent thermal drone surveys in Manakoakora and Ihofa produced findings important enough to be accepted for presentation at ATBC 2026 in Xishuangbanna, China, one of the leading international conferences in tropical biology and conservation.

What the surveys covered

Using a Mavic 3T thermal drone, surveys were conducted in two forests in northeastern Madagascar: a privately owned site in Manakoakora and a community-managed forest in Ihofa.

What was found

Across both sites, multiple lemur species were detected spanning a wide range of body sizes. To the best of current knowledge, this is the first report from Madagascar of lemurs of such varied body sizes detected through thermal drone surveys.

Why this matters

Non-invasive monitoring

Thermal drone surveys can help monitor biodiversity without cutting new trails into the forest, reducing disturbance while extending access to less monitored sites.

Visibility for overlooked forests

The work helps bring attention to forests that receive limited formal conservation monitoring and limited international visibility.

Next technical step

A computer vision model is also being developed to improve individual detection from survey footage and strengthen the future monitoring workflow.

Why ATBC 2026 matters

Presenting at ATBC 2026 puts this research in front of the international conservation and tropical biology community. That matters not only for recognition, but for future collaboration, credibility, and visibility for forests that need more attention.
