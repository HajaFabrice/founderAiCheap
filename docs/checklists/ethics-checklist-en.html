3-Minute Ethics Checklist for Conservation Communication

Use this before every public post, thread, caption, or project update. It is not a branding tool. It is a safety filter for sharing real conservation work without exposing species, data, or people carelessly.

6 checks 3 minutes EN version Print-friendly
Run all six checks. If one answer is uncertain, do not publish yet. Rewrite, generalize, delay, or ask for another pair of eyes.
01

Species location sensitivity

Does this post reveal anything that could help someone locate a nest, roost, den, micro-site, or individual animal? GPS points, named sub-sites, trail references, and recognizable habitat clues all count.

02

Unpublished data protection

Are you sharing exact numbers, statistical values, or results from work that has not yet been formally published or cleared for public discussion? Save the precise figures for the paper or approved report.

03

Community and partner protection

Are any community members, local technicians, field assistants, or partner organizations identifiable without explicit consent? Use broader descriptions if the detail is not essential to the lesson.

04

Scientific integrity

Are you clearly separating what you observed, what you interpret, and what you conclude? A good public post can still be simple without collapsing evidence and inference into one claim.

05

Sensitive timing

Is the project in a live field season, negotiation, regulatory review, or fragile partnership phase? Some details are not unsafe forever, but they are unsafe to share now.

06

Tone and confidence calibration

Does the post sound professional, honest, and specific, or does it drift into overclaiming, vagueness, or performative modesty? Credibility is damaged both by hype and by false smallness.

Why It Matters

Conservation communication is different from generic content creation. The wrong detail can expose species, communities, research partners, or projects. This checklist exists to make that risk visible before you hit publish.

Quick Response Guide

If one answer is “yes”

Remove, generalize, or delay the detail. The story is almost never worth the preventable risk.

If one answer is “I’m not sure”

Pause and get another reviewer. Uncertainty is already enough to justify a safer version.

If you still want visibility

Keep the lesson, the field reality, and the human insight. Drop the sensitive specifics.
