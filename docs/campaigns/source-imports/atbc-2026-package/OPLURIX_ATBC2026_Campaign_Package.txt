A thermal drone does not disturb the forest.

It flies at 70 metres. The animals do not hear it. No trails are cut. No animals are handled. And it sees what a ground observer cannot: heat signatures through canopy gaps, early in the morning, before solar heating raises the background temperature.

In Manakoakora and Ihofa — two forests in northeastern Madagascar that receive limited formal monitoring — we detected lemur signatures across a wide range of body sizes in a single survey series.

That result matters not because it proves something definitive. It matters because it suggests these methods can reveal biological activity in forests that would otherwise go unmonitored for years.

We are taking this to ATBC 2026 in June — the leading international conference for tropical biology — to put it in front of the researchers, funders, and institutions who work on exactly this problem.
