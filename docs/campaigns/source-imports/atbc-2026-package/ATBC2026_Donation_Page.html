The research
The fieldwork

Forests that receive
almost no formal monitoring.

Manakoakora and Ihofa — two forests in northeastern Madagascar — are biologically rich and systematically overlooked. Until we surveyed them, there was almost no recent detection record for the species living in them.

Using a Mavic 3T thermal drone at 70 metres altitude, we detected multiple lemur species across a wide range of body sizes. No trails cut into the forest. No animals disturbed. Early morning, before solar heating closes the detection window.

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report from Madagascar of lemurs spanning such a range of body sizes detected through thermal drone surveys.

We are also developing a computer vision model to automate detection from survey footage — so forests like these can be monitored at scale by local teams with fewer resources.

I am Haja Fabrice Razafindrabe Maminiaina — a Malagasy field biologist and conservation technologist. Madagascar holds approximately 5% of the world's species on 0.4% of its land surface. I grew up surrounded by that biodiversity. I chose to study it.

I am building OPLURIX — an ecological intelligence and MRV platform that connects biodiversity field data to conservation finance. The core of that platform is ERIS (Ecological Integrity Scoring System) — an open methodology for scoring ecological integrity from drone and field survey data, designed to make under-monitored forests visible to the capital that could protect them.

The forests I survey are not the famous ones. They are the ones that receive almost no attention. This trip is how their data reaches the international community that shapes what gets funded and what gets monitored.
