### Post 2 EN

- Schedule: Week 1, Wednesday, 16:00 EAT
- Type: Educational
- Hashtags: `#SciComm #Conservation #Ecology #FieldBiology #ResearchImpact`

A zero in your datasheet is not a decline.

In Kirindy forest, as the dry season approached, active lizard observations dropped sharply. Not because the animals disappeared, but because ectotherms become less active as conditions change, and cryptic species become harder to detect.

The paper on this is still under review. But the methodological lesson is clear and shareable now:

Low detection in one survey period does not confirm low abundance. It confirms low detection. Those are different statements. They require different responses.

One says: the population may be at risk.
The other says: our method may be reaching its limits for this species at this time of year.

Conflating them produces bad conservation decisions.

The honest communication is not "this species is rare here." It is "our current method detected few individuals during this period, and here is why that matters for how we interpret the data."

Have you had to make that distinction in your own reporting? What made it difficult to communicate?

#SciComm #Conservation #Ecology #FieldBiology #ResearchImpact

### Post 5 EN

- Schedule: Week 2, Wednesday, 16:00 EAT
- Type: Proof / Technical
- Hashtags: `#FieldToAI #ConservationTech #MadagascarBiodiversity #FieldBiology #AfricanScience`

Averages can hide the most interesting biology.

In Kirindy forest, Zonosaurus karsteni individuals showed striking variation in their foraging behaviour, from extremely sedentary to extremely active, within the same species, same forest, same study period.

That is the finding worth sitting with: not the average, but the range.

For Z. laticaudatus, we could only observe two individuals during the entire study. Two. The sample size makes any broader characterisation of the species scientifically indefensible. What we can say is what those two individuals did. Nothing more.

This is one of the hardest things to communicate honestly in field herpetology, the difference between what your data shows and what your data means. Two individuals adopting different strategies tells you something real and interesting. It does not tell you what the species does.

Reporting only what the data supports, rather than what you wish it showed, is not a weakness in scientific communication. It is the foundation of credibility.

Has your data ever told you something interesting that you could not actually claim? What did you do with it?

Les moyennes peuvent masquer la biologie la plus interessante.

Dans la foret de Kirindy, les individus de Zonosaurus karsteni presentaient une variation frappante dans leur comportement alimentaire, allant de l'extremement sedentaires a l'extremement actifs, au sein de la meme espece, dans la meme foret, pendant la meme periode d'etude.

Vos donnees vous ont-elles deja dit quelque chose d'interessant que vous ne pouviez pas vraiment affirmer ? Qu'en avez-vous fait ?

### Post 4 EN

- Schedule: Week 3, Tuesday, 13:00 EAT
- Type: Educational / checklist capture
- Hashtags: `#SciComm #OpenScience #EcologicalIntelligence #LearnOnLinkedIn #ConservationTech`

You can talk about research before it is published.
Just not about the results.

Three things you CAN share from unpublished work:
1. The question you asked, and why it matters
2. The method you used, and what it cannot capture
3. The challenge you faced, and what you learned from it

Three things to hold until after peer review:
1. Specific numerical findings
2. Statistical claims or comparisons
3. Conservation recommendations derived from preliminary results

For the Zonosaurus study from Kirindy: I can say we studied foraging flexibility in two lizard species in Madagascar's dry deciduous forest. I can say we used focal observation methodology. I can say the fieldwork raised questions we did not expect going in.

I cannot share the foraging indices or the statistical outputs.

That is not hiding science. That is protecting it until the process designed to check it is complete.

Save this checklist. You will need it.

### Post 6 EN

- Schedule: Week 3, Wednesday, 16:00 EAT
- Type: Demo
- Hashtags: `#FieldToAI #SciComm #ConservationTech #EcologicalIntelligence #LearnOnLinkedIn`

From voice memo to published post, 20 minutes.

RAW NOTE, recorded on phone during focal observation, Kirindy forest:
"Zonosaurus karsteni, site CS7, resting on leaf litter. Moved. Stopped. Resting again on leaf litter. Dead branch right there, higher, better view, not used."

That observation kept coming back during analysis. Not because it proved something definitive. Because it raised a question the data kept pointing toward.

Both species, across almost all observations, rested exclusively on leaf litter, even when dead branches were available that would have given sit-and-wait foraging individuals a much better vantage point for detecting approaching prey.

Using Framework 1 (Field Observation -> What It Raises -> Honest Caveat):

"In Kirindy, both study species rested almost exclusively on leaf litter throughout the study, even when elevated dead branches sat unused nearby. For a sit-and-wait forager, a higher perch should mean better prey detection. We documented the pattern. Why they make this choice, consistently, remains an open question."

That is the post. One real observation, honestly framed, with the limitation acknowledged. No inflation needed.

The voice memo took 12 seconds. The post took 18 minutes.

### Post 4 FR

- Schedule: Week 3, Thursday, 13:00 EAT
- Type: Educational / checklist capture
- Hashtags: `#CommunicationScientifique #ScienceOuverte #EcologicalIntelligence #LearnOnLinkedIn #ConservationTech`

Vous pouvez parler de votre recherche avant qu'elle soit publiee.
Juste pas des resultats.

Trois choses que vous POUVEZ partager d'un travail non publie :
1. La question que vous avez posee, et pourquoi elle compte
2. La methode utilisee, et ce qu'elle ne peut pas capturer
3. Le defi rencontre, et ce que vous en avez appris

Trois choses a garder jusqu'apres la revision par les pairs :
1. Les resultats numeriques specifiques
2. Les affirmations ou comparaisons statistiques
3. Les recommandations de conservation tirees de resultats preliminaires

Pour l'etude sur les Zonosaurus de Kirindy : je peux dire qu'on a etudie la flexibilite du mode d'alimentation chez deux especes de lezards dans la foret seche de Madagascar. Je peux dire qu'on a utilise la methode des observations focales. Je peux dire que le travail a souleve des questions auxquelles on ne s'attendait pas.

Je ne peux pas partager les indices de mode d'alimentation ni les resultats statistiques.

Ce n'est pas cacher la science. C'est la proteger jusqu'a ce que le processus concu pour la verifier soit termine.
