Section 1

The Core Promise

A field survey is not an adventure. It is an operational mission with a specific scientific objective, a team of people whose safety is your responsibility, equipment that can fail, logistics that can collapse, weather that does not care about your schedule, and data that is irreplaceable once the season ends.

Most field missions are planned the way field missions have always been planned: a combination of experience, memory, informal WhatsApp coordination, and improvisation. This works — until it does not. A missing permit. A team member who did not know which site to go to. An equipment list that forgot the memory cards. A near-accident because nobody had reviewed the safety protocol since the previous field season.

This guide does not replace field experience. It documents it. It gives you a system for turning what experienced field biologists know intuitively into an explicit, shared, reviewable plan that any team member can follow, any funder can audit, and any new team member can learn from.

Section 2

10 Real Pain Points

01
Planning happens in your head — and then falls apart in the field.

You know the plan. But when a team member arrives at the wrong site, or equipment arrives one day late, or the driver does not know where to go, it becomes clear that the plan was never actually shared — it was only understood by you.

02
Transect or plot design is improvised in the field.

Deciding transect placement, length, and spacing during the field trip rather than before it wastes the first one or two days and compromises the statistical design. Good spatial design requires a map, a research question, and a calculation — none of which should happen in the field for the first time.

03
The equipment list is never complete.

Something critical is always missing. Not because you are careless — because equipment lists are built from memory and memory is imperfect under time pressure. A structured, category-based equipment master list that you update after every field season eliminates this problem.

04
Roles are assumed, not assigned.

"Everyone knows what they need to do" is the most common precursor to nobody doing what needs to be done. Assigned roles with specific responsibilities, written down before departure, prevent the most common coordination failures in field teams.

05
You have no plan for when things go wrong.

Equipment fails. People get sick. Roads are impassable. A community permits access at some sites but not others. Every field mission has a risk register — even if it only exists in the field leader's head. Writing it down before departure means the whole team knows the contingency plan, not just one person.

06
Safety protocols are informal or absent.

In remote field conditions, a snake bite, a vehicle accident, an allergic reaction, or a fall can become life-threatening without a documented emergency protocol. This is not a bureaucratic requirement — it is the difference between a managed emergency and a crisis.

07
Community and permit coordination happens too late.

Permission from community leaders and government authorities takes time. Starting the permitting process one week before departure instead of one month before departure is the single most common reason for delayed or cancelled field seasons.

08
You cannot prove what was planned vs. what was executed.

Funders and institutional partners increasingly require field mission documentation — not just results. A pre-written mission plan with a post-mission comparison is the only way to show the difference between what was planned and what actually happened, and why.

09
Knowledge about the site lives only with the most experienced team member.

If the person who has been to this site before cannot come this season, all institutional knowledge about access routes, camp locations, water sources, and hazards is lost. A site information document solves this.

10
Nothing is learned between field seasons.

After every field mission, there are lessons — what worked, what failed, what to do differently. Without a structured debrief, these lessons live in informal conversations and are forgotten before the next season. The debrief system in Section 13 captures them in a form the next team can use.

Section 4

The 7-Step Mission Planning System

This workflow runs in reverse chronological order — it starts from the mission end date and works backward. Every field mission should start planning at least 6 weeks before departure. 8–10 weeks is better for remote or multi-site missions.

1

DEFINE THE SCIENTIFIC OBJECTIVE AND MINIMUM SUCCESS CRITERIA

Before any logistics, answer: what does this mission need to produce, at minimum, to be considered a success? A field mission with a vague objective produces vague data. One with a specific objective produces a specific dataset.

Objective Definition Questions
  • What is the primary research question this mission will address?
  • What is the minimum dataset that constitutes a successful mission? (e.g., "At least 800 trap-nights of camera trap data across 20 sites" — not "as much data as possible")
  • What are the secondary objectives that are desirable but not critical?
  • What would cause the mission to be aborted or significantly modified?
  • What data must be collected in what format for downstream analysis?

Output: A one-paragraph mission objective statement and a minimum success criteria table. Both go into the Mission Briefing document.

2

DESIGN THE SURVEY LAYOUT

Before departure, not on arrival. Transect placement, camera trap grid design, plot locations, and acoustic monitor sites are scientific decisions that require a map, a sampling theory, and calculations. Use the Transect Design Guide in Section 8 and Prompts 4–6 in the AI Prompt Pack.

Key outputs of this step:

  • A map with all survey locations plotted and labelled
  • GPS coordinates for every transect endpoint, plot corner, camera trap site, and acoustic monitor location
  • A sampling effort table: total transect km, total trap-nights, total acoustic nights, by site
  • A note on the sampling rationale: why this design answers the research question

Output: A survey design map and a sampling effort table. These go into the Mission Briefing document and the grant reporting file.

3

ASSEMBLE THE TEAM AND ASSIGN ROLES

Every person on the mission has a named role with specific daily responsibilities. Roles are not assumed — they are documented before departure.

Standard field team roles
  • Mission Leader: Overall responsibility for safety, schedule, and scientific objectives. Makes all Go/No-Go decisions. Single point of contact for external parties during the mission.
  • Data Manager: Responsible for daily data backup, quality checks, and datasheet completeness. Reviews all data entries at end of each field day.
  • Equipment Officer: Responsible for all equipment — charging, maintenance, inventory, and troubleshooting. Carries the equipment manifest.
  • Safety Officer: Responsible for daily safety briefings, emergency protocol, first aid kit, and emergency communication. Can override Mission Leader on safety grounds.
  • Community Liaison: Manages all interaction with local communities, guides, and authorities. Often a local team member.
  • Field Technicians (variable): Conduct specific survey tasks according to daily assignment sheet.

Small teams often combine roles. The minimum is: one person responsible for safety and one person responsible for data — and these responsibilities should not default to being both the Mission Leader's alone.

4

PERMITS, AUTHORISATIONS, AND COMMUNITY COORDINATION

This step must begin at least 4 weeks before departure. 6–8 weeks for national parks or sites with complex institutional arrangements. Permit delays are the most common cause of wasted fieldwork budget.

Standard permit checklist for Madagascar field research
  • Research permit: Ministry of Environment (MEDD) — 4–8 weeks processing time
  • Protected area access permit: MNP (Madagascar National Parks) if site is inside a national park
  • Specimen/tissue collection permit: if biological samples will be collected
  • Drone flight authorisation: ANAC (civil aviation authority) — 4–8 weeks; separate from research permit
  • Community consent: formal or informal meeting with fokontany (local community authority) leaders
  • Export permit: if any biological material will leave Madagascar

For other countries: adapt the permit types but keep the same lead time principles. Never assume a permit from a previous season is still valid — check before each mission.

5

BUILD THE EQUIPMENT LIST AND LOGISTICS PLAN

Use the Equipment Master Lists in Section 11. Build your mission-specific list by going through each category and checking what you have, what you need to acquire, and what needs servicing before departure. Then plan the logistics: transport, accommodation, resupply schedule, power access, and communication.

Logistics planning checklist
  • Transport: vehicle type, driver contacts, fuel access along route, 4WD requirement, wet season road status
  • Accommodation: camp location with coordinates, water source, latrine, cooking setup
  • Resupply: food for full team for mission duration + 3 days contingency; fuel; battery charging access
  • Communication: satellite phone or PLB for remote sites; emergency contacts list; regular check-in schedule
  • Medical: nearest clinic or hospital with coordinates; first aid kit inventory; team medical conditions documented
  • Data: storage capacity for all data collected; backup plan; data transfer plan at end of mission
6

ASSESS RISKS AND BUILD THE CONTINGENCY PLAN

Every mission has risks. Document them before departure. A risk that is identified in advance has a plan. A risk that is encountered without warning becomes a crisis. Use the Risk Register in Section 9.

The four risk categories for field missions:

  • Safety risks: Personnel injury, wildlife encounter, vehicle accident, medical emergency, evacuation requirement
  • Operational risks: Equipment failure, permit problem, logistics breakdown, weather disruption, site access denied
  • Data risks: Data loss, data corruption, protocol deviation, insufficient sample size
  • Relationship risks: Community tension, institutional conflict, team interpersonal issues
7

WRITE THE MISSION BRIEFING AND DISTRIBUTE

The Mission Briefing is a single document that every team member receives before departure. It is not long — a good mission briefing for a 2-week field survey is 4–6 pages. It contains everything a team member needs to know to do their job safely and effectively without asking the Mission Leader every question.

Use the Mission Briefing Template in Section 5. Use Prompt 1 in the AI Prompt Pack to draft it from your planning notes.

The non-negotiable distribution rule

Every team member receives the Mission Briefing before departure — not at the first camp. Print copies for field use. Save a digital copy on every team device. Send a copy to the institutional contact who is your emergency point of contact.

Section 8

Transect Design Guide

The key principles for designing statistically sound transect, plot, and monitoring layouts before arriving at the field site. These are frameworks, not substitutes for a statistician's review on complex designs.

Line Transect Design — Core Principles

Sampling effort calculation

The minimum sampling effort needed depends on the detection probability of your target species and the population density you are trying to estimate.

Minimum detections needed for distance sampling: n ≥ 60–80 individual detections
Transect length needed = (target detections) ÷ (expected density × effective strip width × 2)

For occupancy modelling: minimum 10 sites × 3 repeat surveys, or 30 sites × 2 surveys, depending on expected occupancy and detection probability. Use the package RPresence or the occupancy design tool at USGS to calculate site number requirements.

Transect Placement Rules

  • Systematic random placement is almost always preferable to convenience placement. Lay a grid over your study area map. Place transect start points randomly within grid cells. This is reproducible and defensible.
  • Avoid placing transects along roads, trails, or streams unless those features are the survey target. Animals use these features differently from the surrounding habitat — transects placed along them produce biased density estimates.
  • Transect spacing should be at least 2× the expected detection distance to avoid double-counting individuals across adjacent transects.
  • Transects should be perpendicular to the main habitat gradient (e.g., perpendicular to a stream, perpendicular to an elevation contour) to ensure they sample the full range of habitat variation.
  • Record all transect start and end points with GPS before beginning any survey. Transect endpoints that are determined in the field are inconsistently replicable.

Three Non-Negotiable Daily Rituals

These three things happen every single day. No exceptions.
  • Morning safety brief before any team leaves camp — even on days when nothing seems risky. Habits that form on easy days hold on hard days.
  • Evening data backup to at least two devices before anyone sleeps. Data lost overnight cannot be recovered.
  • Evening debrief — 15 minutes, Mission Leader facilitates. What happened. What changes. Tomorrow's plan distributed.

Equipment Master Lists

Complete category-by-category equipment lists for a standard multi-method biodiversity survey. Mark each item as HAVE / NEED / N/A for your specific mission.

Survey Equipment

  • Camera traps — units + security cables + padlocks + desiccant packs
  • AudioMoth units — units + weatherproof cases + deployment straps
  • Drone + controller + batteries (3× minimum per session) + ND filters + landing pad
  • GPS units (1 per team + 1 spare) — pre-loaded with all waypoints
  • Rangefinder / laser distance meter (for transect surveys)
  • Binoculars (1 pair per observer)
  • Headlamps (2 per person) + spare batteries
  • Field datasheets — 150% of expected quantity + waterproof clipboard + pencils
  • Flagging tape for transect marking
  • Measuring tape (50m) for plot surveys
  • Pitfall traps / Sherman traps / mist nets if applicable

Data Collection and Storage

  • Memory cards — 50% more than calculated minimum. Class 10, 32 GB, FAT32 formatted
  • External SSDs (2× 1 TB minimum for large datasets)
  • Laptop or tablet for field data entry and backup
  • USB card reader
  • Waterproof cases for all electronics
  • Printed protocol reference cards for each survey method
  • Printed species identification guides for target taxa

Power and Charging

  • Solar panel (minimum 60W for remote sites) + charge controller
  • Power bank (20,000 mAh minimum, 2× for team of 6+)
  • Multi-port USB charging hub
  • Drone battery charging hub
  • AA batteries — Energizer Lithium for cold/humid, alkaline acceptable in temperate conditions
  • AAA batteries — for headlamps
  • Voltage adapter if team includes international members

Safety and Medical

  • First aid kit — complete, checked for expiry before departure
  • Snake bite kit (pressure bandage — not tourniquet)
  • Personal medication for each team member documented with Data Manager
  • Malaria prophylaxis if applicable for region
  • Water purification (tablets + filter — both)
  • PLB (Personal Locator Beacon) or satellite communicator for remote sites
  • Emergency whistle (every team member)
  • Emergency contact list — laminated, carried by every team member
  • Vehicle emergency kit: tyre repair, tow rope, basic tools, fuel can

Camp and Accommodation

  • Tents (1 per 2 people + 1 extra for equipment storage)
  • Sleeping bags rated for minimum expected temperature
  • Cooking equipment + fuel + food for [N] days + 3-day contingency
  • Water containers (10L per person per day minimum)
  • Latrine spade + hygiene kit
  • Mosquito nets + repellent + sun protection

Documentation and Admin

  • All permits — originals + 3 photocopies each
  • Team ID documents — photocopies held by Mission Leader
  • Mission Briefing — printed copies for all team members
  • Emergency contact list — laminated, distributed
  • Vehicle and fuel budget + petty cash for contingencies
  • Equipment log book

Section 14

Quick Start — Plan Your Next Mission

8 Weeks Before Departure

  • Define the scientific objective and minimum success criteria (Step 1).
  • Identify the team. Assign roles. Confirm availability.
  • Submit all permit applications. This is the most time-critical step.
  • Begin community engagement — notify the fokontany of planned arrival dates.

6 Weeks Before Departure

  • Design the survey layout using Section 8 and Prompts 4–6. Produce the survey map.
  • Build the equipment list using Section 11. Order or borrow missing items.
  • Book transport. Confirm driver and vehicle.
  • Use Prompt 7 to build the Risk Register.

4 Weeks Before Departure

  • Follow up on permit applications. Escalate if no response received.
  • Complete the day-by-day itinerary using Prompt 10.
  • Use Prompt 8 to write the community engagement plan.
  • Run the Equipment Master List against what you have. Order everything missing.

2 Weeks Before Departure

  • Test all equipment. Camera traps, AudioMoths, drone, GPS. Document any failures.
  • Use Prompt 1 to draft the Mission Briefing from your planning notes.
  • Distribute Mission Briefing to all team members. Collect acknowledgements.
  • Print Emergency Protocol cards. Laminate. Distribute.

Day Before Departure

  • Equipment Officer: final equipment check against manifest.
  • Data Manager: confirm all datasheets printed, memory cards formatted, backup devices ready.
  • Mission Leader: confirm permit originals and copies in mission pack.
  • All team: confirm personal medical supplies, ID documents, emergency contacts.

Within 7 Days of Return

  • Complete the Debrief Report using Prompt 11 and the template in Section 13.
  • Update the Site Information Document with new knowledge from this mission.
  • Implement any immediate equipment replacements or protocol changes identified in the debrief.
  • Archive mission documentation: briefing, datasheets, debrief report, permits.
