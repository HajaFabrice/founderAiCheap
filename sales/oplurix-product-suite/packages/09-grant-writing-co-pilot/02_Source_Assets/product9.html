Section 1

The Core Promise

Grant writing is a skill. Like any skill, it can be learned, practiced, and systematised. Most conservation professionals write grant applications the wrong way: starting with their science, writing everything they know, and hoping the reviewer will find the funding case somewhere in the methodology section. Reviewers do not look for the funding case. They expect it to be made clearly, early, and with specific evidence.

The difference between a funded and an unfunded application of equivalent scientific merit is almost always a communication difference, not a science difference. The funded application made a clear argument. The unfunded one described a project. The funded application told the reviewer exactly what problem was being solved, why existing approaches were insufficient, and what specifically would change as a result of this funding. The unfunded one started with the study site and never quite explained why this mattered.

This guide gives you the system. It works for Rufford Small Grants. It works for the WWF Russell E. Train Fellowship. It works for GEF Small Grants and the WCS Fonseca Fund. It is built from the actual structures and reviewer criteria used by each funder — not from generic grant-writing advice.

Section 2

10 Real Pain Points

01
You start writing without knowing what the funder actually values.

Every major conservation funder has a specific theory of change, a specific geographic or thematic focus, and specific reviewer criteria that are publicly available. Most applicants never read them carefully. This guide does that work for you — for each major funder.

02
Your project description is too long, too technical, and too methodology-heavy.

Reviewers spend an average of 15–25 minutes on each application in a competitive round. If the first two paragraphs do not make a compelling case, the rest does not get read carefully. Scientists write for scientific peers. Grant applications are written for programme officers and reviewers who may not be specialists in your taxon.

03
Your budget is vague, unjustified, or internally inconsistent.

A budget line that says "field equipment: $5,000" without specification is a red flag. A budget that does not add up exactly is an immediate credibility problem. A budget that asks for costs without explaining what they enable is an incomplete application — even if the science is excellent.

04
You do not know how to write about impact without overclaiming.

Conservation funders want evidence of impact potential. Overstating it ("this project will save the species") destroys credibility with experienced reviewers. Understating it ("we hope to contribute to the conservation of this habitat") fails to motivate funding. The calibrated impact statement — specific, achievable, and consequential — is a learnable skill.

05
You do not present your team compellingly.

Most grant applications present the team as a list of names and degrees. Funders are investing in people as much as projects. The team section must answer: why are these specific people uniquely positioned to succeed with this specific project in this specific place?

06
You cannot write a project that is both scientifically rigorous and accessible to a non-specialist reviewer.

The methodological rigour must be present — but it must not dominate. A well-structured grant application leads with the problem and the solution, then supports it with enough methodology to demonstrate credibility without losing a non-specialist reviewer in the details.

07
You are applying to the wrong funders for your project type.

A project seeking MRV-grade biodiversity data for conservation finance is not the same kind of project as a community conservation awareness programme. They require different funders, different narratives, and different evidence of impact. Applying with the wrong framing to the wrong funder wastes a cycle and wastes the application fee where applicable.

08
You have no system for reusing application sections across multiple funders.

The project description you write for Rufford can be adapted for WWF with 2 hours of work — not rewritten from scratch. The budget justification you write for GEF can anchor the Rufford equipment narrative. A modular writing system makes every application faster and every submission stronger because it is built on tested language.

09
You do not know how to respond to reviewer feedback after a rejection.

Most grant applications are rejected. Most can be improved and resubmitted. Reviewers almost always provide feedback — and that feedback, read carefully and responded to systematically, converts a rejected application into a funded one. This guide includes a rebuttal and revision framework.

10
You write in a panic close to the deadline every time.

Grant applications written in the final 48 hours are almost always worse than applications written over three weeks. The application calendar in Section 12 gives you a submission-ready timeline that starts 6 weeks before every deadline — not 2 days before.

Section 4

The 6-Step Application System

1

FUNDER-PROJECT FIT ASSESSMENT

Before writing anything, confirm that this funder is the right match for this project at this stage. A mismatched application wastes a review cycle and damages the relationship with the funder for future applications.

Fit Assessment Questions
  • Does the funder's geographic focus include your study area? (Some funders are explicitly regional.)
  • Does the funder's thematic priority match your project? (Species conservation vs. community-based vs. technology-focused vs. policy-focused)
  • Is the grant amount within the appropriate range for your project scale? (A $5,000 project competing against $18,000 ceiling grants looks underpowered.)
  • Is the project stage appropriate? (Some funders prefer pilot/proof-of-concept. Others prefer scaling proven approaches.)
  • Have you or your institution received funding from this funder before? (First-time applicants sometimes need to build a track record with smaller grants first.)
  • Can you meet all eligibility requirements? (Individual vs. institutional applicant, nationality restrictions, PhD student restrictions)

Use Prompt 1 in the AI Prompt Pack to run a systematic funder-project fit assessment before committing to an application.

2

READ THE FUNDER'S CURRENT PRIORITIES

Every major funder publishes their current strategic priorities, recent funded project summaries, and reviewer criteria. Read all three before writing. The recent funded projects are the most valuable — they show you exactly what language, what scale, and what impact evidence the funder is currently rewarding.

  • Download the current application guidelines — not the version from two years ago.
  • Read the funded project summaries from the last two cycles. Note the language used to describe impact.
  • Identify the specific reviewer criteria, if published. These are the scoring rubric — write to them explicitly.
  • Note the word/character limits for every section. Exceeding them disqualifies applications at some funders.
3

BUILD THE ARGUMENT SPINE BEFORE WRITING

Every successful grant application makes one coherent argument. The argument is not "here is my project." The argument is "here is a specific problem, here is why existing approaches have not solved it, here is what specifically we will do, and here is the evidence that it will work." Write this argument in six sentences before writing any section of the application.

The Six-Sentence Argument
  1. The specific conservation problem at the specific place. (Not global biodiversity — this forest, this species, this threat.)
  2. Why it matters — what is at stake if nothing changes. (Specific, not generic.)
  3. Why the problem persists — what is missing that keeps it unsolved. (Data? Funding? Method? Infrastructure?)
  4. What your project does — in one sentence a non-specialist understands. (The solution.)
  5. Why you — the specific evidence that this team can deliver. (Not just credentials — field evidence.)
  6. What specifically changes — the measurable outcome of this funding. (Not "improved conservation" — a specific, verifiable change.)

If you cannot complete all six sentences before writing, your project concept is not ready. The six-sentence argument is the foundation every section is built on.

4

WRITE SECTION BY SECTION — IN ORDER

Write the sections of the application in the order listed in the framework for your specific funder (Section 6). Do not write the budget before the narrative — the narrative determines the budget. Do not write the timeline before the methodology — the methodology determines the timeline.

Use the AI Prompt Pack (Section 7) for every section. The prompts are designed to take your raw project notes and produce a first draft that you edit — not a final draft you submit unread. Every AI output requires review for scientific accuracy and for fit with your specific project.

5

APPLY THE REVIEWER PSYCHOLOGY CHECKLIST

Before finalising any section, run it through the reviewer psychology framework in Section 8. Reviewers are reading 30–80 applications in a compressed review period. They are looking for specific things in specific places. Give them what they are looking for — do not make them search for it.

The five questions every reviewer asks
  • "Do I understand what this project does within the first paragraph?" If no: rewrite the opening.
  • "Do I believe this team can actually deliver this?" If no: strengthen the team section and evidence.
  • "Is this the right scale for this funder?" If no: adjust the scope or choose a different funder.
  • "Is the budget credible and justified?" If no: add specific justification to each line item.
  • "Does this fit what we are currently funding?" If no: reframe the narrative to align with current priorities.
6

EXTERNAL REVIEW AND FINAL SUBMISSION

Every application should be read by at least one person who is not familiar with the project before submission. Ask them three questions: (1) What does this project do? (2) Why does it matter? (3) Why should this team be funded? If their answers match your intent, the application is ready. If not, revise.

The single most common submission error

Submitting without reading the application aloud. A single read-aloud takes 20–30 minutes. It catches: sentences that are too long to follow, jargon that is not explained, logical gaps between sections, and claims that sound confident internally but vague when spoken. Every submission-ready application has been read aloud at least once.

Section 6

Section-by-Section Narrative Frameworks

Detailed writing frameworks for each section of a conservation grant application. These work for all five funders above with the adaptations noted for each funder in Section 5.

Framework 1 — Project Summary / Executive Statement

The project summary is the most important section. Reviewers read it first and many form their impression before reading the rest. It must stand alone — a reader with no prior context should understand the project, its purpose, and its significance from the summary alone.

Project Summary Structure — 150–200 words
  1. Sentence 1–2: The conservation problem at the specific site. Specific species, specific threat, specific geography. No generalisations.
  2. Sentence 3: Why this problem has not been solved — the gap.
  3. Sentence 4–5: What this project does — the solution in plain language.
  4. Sentence 6: The primary method — one sentence, accessible to a non-specialist.
  5. Sentence 7–8: The specific outputs — what the funding produces.
  6. Sentence 9: The conservation impact — what changes as a result.
Weak summary
"Madagascar is one of the most biodiverse countries in the world but faces severe threats from deforestation and habitat loss. This project will conduct a comprehensive biodiversity survey using multiple methods to assess the conservation status of the Ihofa forest and provide data to support conservation decision-making and management planning in the region."
Strong summary
"The Ihofa forest holds 23% of known Malagasy mammal species in 12,000 hectares of unprotected dry deciduous forest. At current encroachment rates, the forest will lose connectivity with the next viable block before 2032 — isolating three Critically Endangered lemur populations with no dispersal route. This project produces the first field-verified ecological integrity score for this forest, combining lemur transect surveys with thermal drone mapping and acoustic monitoring, in the specific format required for biodiversity credit certification. The output enables conservation finance access for Ihofa — a pathway to sustained forest protection not dependent on grant cycles."

Framework 2 — Conservation Problem / Background

The background section builds the case that this problem is real, specific, and serious enough to warrant funding. It is not a literature review. It is an argument.

Conservation Problem Structure — 400–600 words
  1. The site: Specific geography, habitat type, protection status. Where is this happening? One paragraph.
  2. The ecological significance: Why does this site matter for biodiversity conservation? What species, what endemism level, what ecosystem services? One paragraph.
  3. The threat: Specific, quantified, and localised. Deforestation rate (hectares per year or percentage). Threat timeline. Not "threats include deforestation and hunting" — one primary threat, described specifically. One paragraph.
  4. The gap: Why has this threat not been adequately addressed? What data, infrastructure, or funding is missing? This paragraph makes your project necessary — not just useful. One paragraph.
  5. The urgency: What happens if nothing changes in the next 5 years? Specific and honest. One or two sentences.
Worked example — conservation problem (ERIS / Ihofa context)
The Ihofa forest in Alaotra-Mangoro region covers approximately 12,000 hectares of mid-altitude humid forest in an unprotected landscape. A 2024–2025 multi-method biodiversity inventory documented 47 vertebrate species, of which 38 are Malagasy endemics and 6 are classified as Critically Endangered under IUCN criteria, including Indri indri — the world's largest lemur and a flagship species with no captive population. The primary threat is agricultural encroachment from the forest's southern and eastern margins. Remote sensing analysis shows a 3.2% annual reduction in forest cover along these edges over the 2015–2023 period. At this rate, the forest connectivity to the Angavo block — the next viable forest to the north — will be severed within 8 to 10 years. Once severed, the isolated lemur populations face long-term genetic degradation with no viable dispersal corridor. The most critical gap preventing effective protection is the absence of a verified, auditable ecological data product that conservation finance instruments can act on. Biodiversity credit markets and REDD+ instruments require standardised, independently verifiable ecological scores. No such score currently exists for Ihofa. Without it, the forest cannot access the recurring revenue streams that would make its protection economically viable — and conservation grant funding alone cannot sustain indefinite protection of 12,000 hectares.

Framework 3 — Objectives and Methods

This section must satisfy two audiences simultaneously: the specialist reviewer who checks methodological rigour, and the non-specialist reviewer who must understand what is being done and why. Lead with the objectives — clear, numbered, achievable. Follow with the methods — detailed enough to demonstrate credibility, concise enough to remain readable.

Objectives and Methods Structure
  • Objectives: 3–4 numbered objectives. Each begins with an active verb (assess, establish, produce, develop, deploy). Each is specific enough to be verifiable as achieved or not achieved at project end.
  • Methods per objective: For each objective, one paragraph describing: what you will do, how you will do it, who will do it, and how you will know it has succeeded.
  • Sampling design: For field survey objectives — number of sites, transect length/spacing, trap-nights, survey duration. Numbers that demonstrate you have calculated what is needed for statistical validity.
  • Analysis approach: One sentence per objective on how the data will be analysed. Use accessible language — "occupancy modelling" needs one sentence of explanation; "single-species maximum entropy modelling" does not.
  • Limitations: Acknowledge one or two realistic limitations and how you will manage them. This builds reviewer trust — it shows you are thinking carefully, not overselling.

Framework 4 — Benefits to Conservation

This section answers: so what? A project that produces data produces conservation benefit only if the data is used. Reviewers need to see the pathway from data → decision → conservation outcome.

Benefits to Conservation Structure
  • Immediate outputs: What will physically exist at project end? Dataset, report, score, protocol, trained team, published methodology.
  • Direct conservation benefits: How do those outputs change conservation decisions or actions? Be specific — who will use the output and how?
  • Capacity building: What skills and knowledge remain after the project? In whom? How will they apply it?
  • Wider applicability: Can the methodology be replicated at other sites? By other teams? This multiplies the funder's investment.
  • Sustainability beyond the grant: How will the conservation benefit persist after the funding ends? A project that requires perpetual external funding to maintain its benefit is a weaker case than one that establishes sustainable local capacity or self-financing mechanisms.

Framework 5 — Budget Narrative

The budget is not a spreadsheet attached to the application. It is an argument. Every line item tells the reviewer: "This cost enables this specific output." An unjustified budget line is an unanswered question that weakens the entire application.

Budget Narrative Rules
  • Every line item over $500 gets a justification sentence: what it buys, why it is needed for this specific project, and why this cost level is appropriate.
  • Equipment costs: specify model, quantity, unit cost, and total. Do not write "camera traps: $2,000" — write "10 × Bushnell Core camera traps at $180/unit = $1,800 for 30 camera stations × 30-night minimum deployment to achieve occupancy estimates."
  • Personnel costs: specify role, daily rate, and number of days. Explain why each person is essential to the specific project deliverables.
  • Contingency: 10–15% is standard and expected. Explain briefly that this covers currency fluctuation, equipment failure, and unexpected logistics costs.
  • Never round numbers too aggressively — "exactly $15,000" with no cents looks less credible than "$14,780 + $1,220 contingency = $16,000."
  • Show any co-funding or in-kind contribution explicitly. A $15,000 Rufford application with $5,000 in Techni-Drones in-kind (drone time, staff support) is effectively a $20,000 project at $15,000 cost — that is a strong value proposition.

Section 8

Reviewer Psychology Guide

Understanding how grant reviewers read applications is as important as understanding what to write. This section is based on published research on grant review behaviour and on feedback patterns from major conservation funders.

How Reviewers Read

  • They read the summary first and form an impression. That impression affects how generously they read everything else. A strong summary primes a reviewer to find merit in the details. A weak summary primes them to look for problems.
  • They are reading 30–80 applications in a compressed review period. They are looking for reasons to shortlist or eliminate. Make their job easy — put the most important information in the most obvious place.
  • They look for the funding case, not the project description. Many applications describe a project without making the case for why this specific project deserves funding at this specific moment. The funding case is: this problem is real → these existing approaches are insufficient → this project fills the gap → these specific people can do it → this funder's money makes it possible.
  • They notice what is missing. An application that does not mention the limitations of the proposed methodology looks naïve. One that does not acknowledge community engagement looks extractive. One that does not explain sustainability looks short-sighted. Acknowledge these things before the reviewer has to note them as weaknesses.
  • They remember the last thing they read. The concluding sentences of each section — and especially the final paragraph of the application — carry disproportionate weight. End strong.

The Six Reviewer Scoring Criteria — How to Address Each

CriterionWhat reviewers look forHow to address it explicitly
Scientific merit / rigourIs the methodology appropriate? Are sample sizes justified? Are limitations acknowledged?State sample size calculations explicitly. Name the statistical approach. Acknowledge one or two limitations with management strategies.
Conservation relevanceDoes this produce conservation outcomes, not just scientific outputs?Connect every output to a specific conservation decision or action. Name who will use the data and how.
Applicant credibilityCan this team actually deliver what it promises?Lead with field-specific experience at this site or with these methods. Reference prior outputs (publications, reports, previous funded projects).
Value for moneyIs the budget proportional to the expected outputs?Calculate and state the cost-per-output explicitly. Show any co-funding or in-kind contributions.
SustainabilityWhat persists after the grant ends?Describe institutional capacity, trained local staff, established methodology, or financing pathway that continues without external grant funding.
FeasibilityIs the timeline realistic? Are the objectives achievable in the grant period?Show that the timeline accounts for permit lead times, field season constraints, and analysis time. Do not overpromise.

Rebuttal and Revision Guide

Most conservation grants are competitive. Most strong applications are rejected at least once. A well-handled rejection is a funded resubmission in the next cycle.

Reading Reviewer Feedback

  • Read it twice before reacting. The first read after a rejection triggers defensiveness. The second read, a day later, identifies what is actually actionable.
  • Classify every criticism as: VALID (the reviewer identified a real weakness) / MISUNDERSTANDING (the information was in the application but communicated poorly) / DISAGREEMENT (the reviewer has a different scientific or strategic view).
  • VALID criticisms are gifts. They tell you exactly what to improve. A reviewer who says "the sampling design is not adequately justified" has given you the revision instruction.
  • MISUNDERSTANDINGS are communication failures — which means they are your responsibility to fix, not the reviewer's failure to read carefully. If a reviewer missed something important, it was not prominent enough.
  • DISAGREEMENTS require acknowledgment, not capitulation. If a reviewer thinks your budget is too large and you have good reasons for each line item, defend them with evidence in the revision — do not simply reduce the budget to please the reviewer.

The Revision Protocol

  1. List every reviewer criticism in a table with: criticism text / classification (valid/misunderstanding/disagreement) / intended revision.
  2. For VALID criticisms: make the specific improvement. Note what changed and where.
  3. For MISUNDERSTANDINGS: move the relevant information to a more prominent position. Often this means adding it to the summary or the opening paragraph of the relevant section.
  4. For DISAGREEMENTS: add a clear, evidence-based response. Acknowledge the reviewer's perspective, then explain why your approach is appropriate for this specific project and context.
  5. Write a cover letter for the resubmission that clearly explains what changed and why. Reviewers who see a revision round appreciate knowing their feedback was taken seriously.

Quick Start — Your Next Application in 6 Weeks

Today (Day 1)

  • Identify your target funder from this guide. Read their funder profile (Section 5) in full.
  • Download the current application guidelines from the funder's website. Not a cached version — the current one.
  • Run Prompt 1 (funder-project fit assessment). If the result is WEAK FIT, choose a different funder.
  • Mark your deadline in your calendar. Mark Week 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 milestones from the calendar (Section 10).

This Week (Week 6)

  • Run Prompt 2 (six-sentence argument). Write it out. Test it: can you explain it to a non-specialist friend in 60 seconds? If not, revise.
  • Send letters of support request to all institutional partners. These take the longest to arrive and cannot be chased at the last moment.
  • Build a rough budget. You do not need exact numbers yet — but you need to know whether the project fits within the grant ceiling.

Weeks 5–4

  • Write conservation problem section (Prompt 4). This is the most important narrative section.
  • Write objectives (Prompt 5) and methods (Prompt 6).
  • Build the detailed budget and write the budget narrative (Prompt 8).
  • Write the team section (Prompt 9).

Weeks 3–2

  • Write the project summary LAST (Prompt 3).
  • Run the reviewer psychology checklist against every section (Section 8).
  • Send to one external reviewer. Ask the three questions.
  • Revise based on their feedback.

Week 1 — Final

  • Read the complete application aloud. Fix anything that sounds unclear or forced.
  • Check every word count. Trim any section over the limit.
  • Add up the budget again from scratch.
  • Verify all attachments are attached and formatted correctly.
  • Submit at least 24 hours before the deadline.
